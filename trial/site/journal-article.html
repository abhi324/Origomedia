The quiet death of the
follower count.

By Aryan L. · Founder, Origo

The first signal came from a skincare founder we'd been working with for nine months. Her CFO had started asking, in their weekly finance review, about "content saves per ₹ spent" instead of reach. Nobody on the growth team had asked for the metric. It had appeared, quietly, in the spreadsheet as a column, and then it was the only column anyone looked at.

That was six months ago. Since then, four of the seven beauty brands on our roster have moved follower count out of their creator-marketing KPIs and into a "vanity" column on a separate sheet. One brand deleted the column entirely.

What happened?

Nothing dramatic. Platforms got better. Algorithms got more honest about surfacing content from outside your follower graph. Creators' reach decoupled from their follower count. The correlation between "I follow you" and "I'll see your post" collapsed to something like 15%.

So if you can't count on followers to see a creator's post, what's the follower count actually measuring?

A follower count is a receipt from a moment that has already passed. It tells you who once found a creator interesting enough to tap a button.

That receipt is useful — don't get us wrong. A creator with 200,000 followers has survived a kind of popularity test. But the signal a brand actually wants (will this creator's content move my customer?) is a different signal. It lives in saves, shares, DMs, replies, and the cadence at which returning viewers come back.

The metrics that are replacing it

In the 200 beauty and skincare campaigns we've run, four metrics have proven to be materially predictive of downstream revenue. They are, in rough order of weight:

1. Save rate per thousand views. The save is the most undervalued interaction in creator marketing. It is the digital equivalent of keeping a magazine tear — intentional, private, and usually followed by a purchase decision days later.

2. Reply-to-comment ratio. A creator who replies to their own comments builds a room. The room is the unit of trust a brand actually borrows from.

3. Returning viewer rate. People come back to creators they like. Platforms know this and increasingly expose it. If less than 20% of a creator's viewers are returning, you are renting audience, not inheriting it.

4. Share-to-close ratio. The number of people who sent a creator's post to someone else, divided by the number of people who then engaged with that share. This is the closest proxy we have to word-of-mouth intensity.

What this means for brands

Stop filtering by follower count. Or if you must, stop doing it first. A roster filtered by save rate looks completely different from a roster filtered by follower count. It tends to skew smaller, younger, and surprisingly category-specific.

It also costs less. A creator with 24,000 followers and a save rate of 9% will move more revenue, in most beauty launches we've seen, than a creator with 240,000 followers and a save rate of 0.6%. And they'll do it for roughly a sixth of the rate.

What this means for creators

Your audience is the product. Treat it that way. Reply to comments. Say no to brands that are wrong for you. Don't chase a follower milestone that costs you a point of save rate to get there.

The creators who will thrive in the next five years are not the ones with the biggest numbers. They are the ones whose numbers mean something — the ones whose rooms are full of people who show up, not wander by.

The follower count isn't dying. It's being demoted — from primary metric to footnote. That's an upgrade for everyone.

— A.L.
